Other than the consonant (pulmonic), vowel and tone charts, there are 4 other sets of symbols: consonants (non-pulmonic); suprasegmentals; diacrtics and other symbols.

Consonants (non-pulmonic) refer to the set of consonantal sounds which articulations do not involve the lungs. To give you an example, if you smack your lips, you would be making a non-pulmonic consonant sound known as a bilabial click. The symbol for that sound is [ʘ]. The making of this sound does not involve the lungs at all.

Suprasegmentals are sounds that are beyond (hence the prefix "supra~") the phones, such as stress or length of articulation. These symbols are used to denote properties of length, break (or absence of break) in linguistic articulation.

Diacritics are symbols that piggyback on the other standard symbols. Diacritics are used to notate tiny variations in the place and manner of articulation. For example, a plosive sound may be articulated with or without a puff of air during the release. To notate the existence of an extra puff of air, the superscripted [ʰ] is added to a plosive, as in the contrast between [t] in "stick" and the [tʰ] in "tick".

Finally, additional IPA symbols are clustered under the miscellaneous group "other symbols".

While all of the above are important in a comprehensive grasp of the IPA, we will not explore them here.
